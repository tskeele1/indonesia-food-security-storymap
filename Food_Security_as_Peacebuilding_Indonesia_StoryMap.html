Food Security as Peacebuilding:
Addressing Structural Violence in Indonesia's Food System

Food insecurity in Indonesia is not a supply deficit. It is structural violence — rooted in colonial history, land inequality, and governance failures. This story map traces that argument from root causes to policy solutions.

Executive summary

Food insecurity in Indonesia is a structural problem, not a supply deficit. It is rooted in colonial agricultural extraction, fragmented land governance, and decades of policy prioritising rice self-sufficiency over nutrition equity. The scale is measurable and the solutions are identifiable.

Applying conflict transformation theory (Galtung; Lederach), this brief argues that equitable food systems are peacebuilding infrastructure. The legal mechanisms and community models needed for reform already exist in Indonesian law — what is missing is the political will to use them. Seven recommendations across Lederach's three levels are offered, each with a specific implementation mechanism, a realistic cost estimate, and measurable progress indicators.

Three historical ruptures

1830 – 1870
Dutch Cultivation System (Cultuurstelsel)
Farmers forced to dedicate land to export crops — sugar, coffee, indigo. Subsistence agriculture displaced. Land inequality embedded for generations. (Elson, 1994; Booth, 1998)
1960s – 1980s
Green Revolution
High-yield rice achieved national self-sufficiency by the 1980s, but created monoculture dependence and excluded smallholders, women, and indigenous farmers from its benefits. Today more than 55% of Indonesian farms still operate on less than 0.5 hectares. (Booth, 2012; BPS, 2023)
1997–98 & 2020
Crises expose fragility
The Asian Financial Crisis and COVID-19 both revealed the same structural weakness: economic shocks translate immediately into food insecurity for the most marginalised households. (Elson, 1994; Booth, 1998)

Structural violence (Galtung, 1969): harm produced not by any individual actor, but by institutional arrangements that systematically prevent people from meeting basic human needs. Colonial agriculture is its paradigmatic form.

Stunting as structural indicator: approximately 21% of Indonesian children under five are stunted, a rate that persists despite national income growth, proving that caloric availability alone does not ensure nutritional security. Eastern vs. western Indonesia

Papua and East Nusa Tenggara face compounding disadvantages: poor transport raises food prices, agricultural productivity is lower, climate variability is higher, and state institutional capacity is weakest precisely where food insecurity is most severe. (Juliannisa et al., 2025; FAO, 2022)

Women and gender inequality

  • Women are central to food production, preparation, and nutrition management — yet face systematic barriers to land ownership, credit, and extension services. (Samputra & Antriyandarti, 2024; FAO, 2023)
  • Pregnant women in food-insecure households face elevated risk of low birth weight and stunting, creating intergenerational transmission of deprivation. (Azrimaidaliza et al., 2025; Black et al., 2013)
  • Under Islamic inheritance law (faraid), women receive half the land share of male heirs. Combined with customary norms, widows and female-headed households frequently hold insufficient land after displacement or disaster.

Evidence base: improving women's access to land, credit, and agricultural extension services is consistently shown to generate strong positive returns for household food security and community well-being. (Samputra & Antriyandarti, 2024; FAO, 2023)

Indigenous communities (Suku Anak Dalam)

Groups like the Suku Anak Dalam depend on forest resources — hunting, gathering, small-scale agriculture — as their primary food system. (Ginting et al., 2025; Margono et al., 2014)

Three converging pressures:

  • Rapid deforestation and plantation expansion reducing forest access
  • No formal legal recognition of territorial rights — AMAN has documented 604 customary land maps covering 6.8 million hectares that remain unrecognised by the state (Arizona & Cohen, 2024)
  • Exclusion from formal development programs and social protection

Smallholders and urban poor

Smallholders face unstable incomes and market volatility. Urban poor face high food prices and limited fresh food access. Both groups are largely invisible in rice self-sufficiency policy frameworks. Indonesia's agricultural sector employs an estimated 38–40 million workers, representing 28–30% of the national workforce, the majority of whom are smallholders. (BPS, 2023; World Bank, 2023)

Intersecting vulnerabilities

These groups are not separate. An indigenous woman farmer in eastern Papua faces all of these burdens simultaneously — land insecurity, reduced inheritance rights, deforestation of her food system, and exclusion from social protection. Conflict transformation demands that policy address intersections, not isolated categories.

Policy

Rice self-sufficiency and Bulog

Indonesia achieved rice self-sufficiency in the 1980s. The state logistics agency (Bulog) administers rice subsidies, import controls, and price stabilisation. These programs have reduced extreme hunger but prioritise caloric sufficiency over dietary diversity and benefit larger operators more than smallholders. (Timmer, 1996; World Bank, 2022)

Policy

Social protection (Rastra / BPNT)

Subsidised rice distribution (Rastra) and non-cash food assistance (BPNT) have played an important role in reducing extreme hunger. However, coverage is uneven — remote and marginalised communities are often underserved. Decentralisation has produced significant variation in implementation quality across provinces. (Aspinall & Fealy, 2003; World Bank, 2022)

Policy

National Strategy on Stunting Reduction (STRANAS)

Indonesia's STRANAS program, coordinated under the Office of the Vice President, is the most significant nutrition-specific policy effort nationally. It integrates interventions across health, water and sanitation, and social protection, and has driven real progress in reducing the 21% stunting rate. However, implementation gaps persist in eastern provinces with weaker institutional capacity, underscoring that national policy architecture alone cannot close regional food security gaps. (Office of the Vice President, 2018; Beal et al., 2018)

Policy gap

ISPO and the customary land paradox

The Indonesian Sustainable Palm Oil (ISPO) certification system (Presidential Regulation No. 44/2020) governs an industry spanning over 16 million hectares and supporting approximately 16 million workers. But ISPO requires formal land title as a prerequisite for certification. An estimated 40–50% of palm oil smallholders currently fall outside ISPO because their customary land rights have never been converted to formal certificates — a process that is prohibitively expensive or legally unavailable for communal land. (Arizona & Cohen, 2024)

Policy gap

EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR, 2023)

The EUDR requires buyers to obtain geolocation and title documentation for all supply chain producers. Smallholders without formal certificates are effectively non-compliant by definition, creating market exclusion with no corresponding ecological benefit. This compounds rather than resolves the ISPO paradox. (European Union, 2023)

Existing mechanism

BPN Ministerial Regulation No. 9/2015

This regulation already provides a formal mechanism for communal tenure recognition in Indonesian law. It remains severely underutilised. Amending ISPO to accept adat community decrees issued under this regulation as equivalent tenure documentation is the most structurally sound reform pathway available. (Government of Indonesia, 2015)

Program model

BRG/BRGM Peat Care Village program

Established under Presidential Regulation No. 1/2016, the Peatland Restoration Agency's Desa Peduli Gambut program integrates restoration goals into village development plans, positions communities as agents rather than recipients, and links ecological outcomes to sustainable livelihoods. It is the most replicable model for community-centred governance in Indonesia. (Government of Indonesia, 2016; Astuti et al., 2021)

Key gap across all policies: no current framework formally recognises the 604 customary land maps covering 6.8 million hectares documented by AMAN. Until this changes, millions of Indonesians remain structurally excluded from the systems designed to protect them.

Galtung: structural violence

Violence does not require a perpetrator with intent. It requires only institutions that consistently prevent people from meeting basic human needs. (Galtung, 1969)

Indonesia's food system is structurally violent when it reliably fails smallholders, women, indigenous communities, and the rural poor — not because any actor wills their harm, but because the institutional arrangements produce it predictably. With 21% of children stunted and 40–50% of palm oil smallholders excluded from ISPO, the scale of structural exclusion is measurable. This framing is consistent with broader peacebuilding literature linking famine and food deprivation to political processes rather than natural scarcity. (de Waal, 2018; Messer & Cohen, 2015)

Food insecurity operates across all levels simultaneously: within households (gender hierarchies and faraid inheritance norms), between communities (rural/urban, indigenous/settler), nationally (governance fragmentation and rice monoculture policy), and globally (climate risk and EUDR market exclusion).

Lederach: three levels of peacebuilding

Durable change requires coordinated action across all three levels simultaneously. Interventions at only one level get captured by existing power arrangements. (Lederach, 1997)

The central argument

Equitable food systems function as peacebuilding infrastructure. When food systems are structured equitably, they reduce material grievances, strengthen social cohesion, and build trust between communities and institutions. Food insecurity is therefore not peripheral to conflict prevention — it is central to it. The legal mechanisms, community models, and institutional frameworks needed for reform already exist in Indonesian law and practice. What is required is the political will to use them.

Seven recommendations organised across Lederach's three levels of change. Each identifies a specific implementation mechanism and the primary political obstacle it faces.

Level 1 — Top-level: national policy reform

1

Reform land tenure through existing legal architecture

Accelerate Reforma Agraria for smallholder and indigenous claims. Amend Presidential Regulation No. 44/2020 (ISPO) to accept adat community decrees issued under BPN Ministerial Regulation No. 9/2015 as equivalent tenure documentation for certification. AMAN's database of 604 customary land maps covering 6.8 million hectares is the evidentiary foundation.

Obstacle: resistance from plantation concession holders and Ministry of Environment & Forestry reluctance to share jurisdictional authority. A tiered ISPO compliance model offers a pragmatic interim pathway.
2

Redesign agricultural policy toward nutrition security

Shift from exclusive rice self-sufficiency toward broader nutrition security. Reform the fertiliser subsidy program (Law No. 19/2013) to equitably reach the more than 55% of farms operating under 0.5 hectares. Invest in market infrastructure, transport, and cold storage in eastern provinces including Papua and East Nusa Tenggara.

Obstacle: political sensitivity of rice self-sufficiency as a national sovereignty narrative and institutional interests of Bulog. Reform should be framed as expanding food security, not dismantling existing structures.

Level 2 — Middle range: institutional and civil society programming

3

Mainstream gender across all food security programs

Mandate gender analysis as a prerequisite for all food security program design and funding. Establish dedicated financing for women's access to land, credit, and extension services, explicitly addressing the barriers created by faraid inheritance norms. Require all agencies to publish sex-disaggregated food insecurity data annually.

Obstacle: institutional resistance within agricultural ministries that have treated smallholder support as gender-neutral, and political sensitivity around religious inheritance law.
4

Protect indigenous food systems through formal territorial recognition

Accelerate formal legal recognition of indigenous territorial rights using Constitutional Court Decision No. 35 (2012) and BPN Regulation No. 9/2015. Require free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) as a binding condition for all development and concession activities on customary land, in line with Indonesia's commitments under the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP).

Obstacle: the Bill on Adat Communities' Rights has stalled since 2014. International development partners can provide technical and financial support to accelerate ethnographic documentation.
5

Strengthen community-based food systems through social capital investment

Support local food networks and cooperative farming arrangements. The Desa Peduli Gambut program (Presidential Regulation No. 1/2016) provides a replicable governance model: community-led, livelihoods-integrated, ecologically grounded. It should be formally evaluated and adapted for food system applications beyond peatland contexts.

Obstacle: high cost of community capacity-building and need for multi-year funding commitments incompatible with short donor project cycles.

Level 3 — Grassroots and local governance

6

Promote climate-resilient and diversified agriculture at the farm level

Scale agroecological farming and diversified cropping through community-based Soil and Water Conservation programs under the Directorate General of Watershed Management. Integrate food security and climate adaptation planning at provincial level. Support smallholders to transition away from monoculture through subsidised access to diverse seed varieties. (IPCC, 2022)

Obstacle: current fertiliser subsidy structures incentivise synthetic nitrogen inputs, creating a financial disincentive to adopt alternative practices. Redirecting a portion of subsidy expenditure toward agroecological transition would address this.
7

Institutionalise participatory governance at every level of food policy

Establish formal multi-stakeholder platforms integrating smallholder, women's, and indigenous voices into food policy design, implementation, and evaluation at national, provincial, and district levels. Build local government institutional capacity through sustained investment in training, data systems, and inter-agency coordination. Commission regular, publicly accessible program evaluations using participatory methodologies.

Obstacle: provinces with the greatest food insecurity needs often have the least institutional capacity. Targeted national investment in these provinces — not uniform programming — is necessary.

Turning recommendations into implementation requires answers to two questions: where will the money come from, and how will we know it is working? This section addresses both.

Potential financing mechanisms

Domestic budget reallocation
Redirect 10-20% of annual fertiliser subsidy expenditure (~$1.5-3B/year) toward smallholder and agroecological transition support. Politically difficult but highest-leverage.
Green Climate Fund
Indonesia's adaptation portfolio includes food security and climate-resilient agriculture. Well-matched to Recommendations 6 and 7.
Bilateral donor programs
Norway (peatland/deforestation finance), Germany (GIZ agricultural programming), and Australia (DFAT regional food security) have existing Indonesia commitments.
Blended finance
Palm oil certification compliance (ISPO + EUDR) creates commercial pressure for supply chain investment. Pair with concessional public finance for smallholder inclusion.
Multilateral development banks
World Bank, ADB, and IFAD all have active Indonesia agricultural portfolios. Recommendations 1, 2, and 6 align with existing lending priorities.
